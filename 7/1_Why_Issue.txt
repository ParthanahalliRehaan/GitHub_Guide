### 🎯 Purpose of Issues
- Issues are **not just bugs** — they’re a way to track **tasks, enhancements, discussions, or questions**.
- They serve as a **public record** of what needs attention, so collaborators (or even just you, if solo) can organize work.

---

### 👤 Can the Owner Create & Resolve Their Own Issue?
Yes, absolutely.  
- **Scenario:** You’re working solo on a repo. You notice a bug in your login function.  
- You open an issue to document it:
  ```bash
  gh issue create --title "Fix login bug" --body "Login fails when username has spaces"
  ```
  Output:
  ```
  ✓ Created issue #12
  https://github.com/Rehaan/my-new-repo/issues/12
  ```

- Later, you fix it and close the issue:
  ```bash
  gh issue close 12
  ```
  Output:
  ```
  ✓ Closed issue #12
  ```

This way, even though you’re both the reporter and resolver, you’ve kept a **clear history** of the problem and its resolution.

---

### 🧩 Why Not Just Skip Issues?
- **Solo dev:** Issues act like a **personal to-do list**. You can track bugs, features, or reminders.
- **Team dev:** Issues are **communication tools**. They let others know what’s being worked on, what’s pending, and who’s responsible.
- **Project management:** Issues can be linked to pull requests, milestones, and projects, creating a structured workflow.

---

### ⚖️ Summary
- **Owner can create and resolve their own issues.**  
- Issues aren’t only for external contributors — they’re for **documentation, planning, and tracking progress**.  
- Think of them as **tickets in a system**: even if you’re the only one using the system, it keeps your work organized and transparent.
