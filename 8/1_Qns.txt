## 🔍 Why rebase usually needs `--force`
- When you **merge**, Git creates a new merge commit but keeps both histories intact. The commit IDs of your old work don’t change.
- When you **rebase**, Git **replays your commits** on top of the remote branch. That means Git creates **new commit objects** with new IDs (even if the content is the same).
- Because the commit IDs are different, your local branch history no longer matches the remote. From Git’s perspective, it looks like you’re trying to rewrite history.
- That’s why a normal `git push` is rejected — Git doesn’t want you to accidentally overwrite someone else’s work. You need `git push --force` to tell Git: *“Yes, I know these are new commits, overwrite the remote with my rebased history.”*

---

## 🎯 So what’s the point of rebase?
The point isn’t to avoid force pushing — it’s to keep your history **linear and clean**:

### Merge history (messy bubble):
```
*   ccce7dd Merge commit
|\
| * 4ec6c65 Remote Dawn 2
* | 10eda99 Local Dawn 2
|/
* 5bf9eb3 Dawn 1
```

### Rebase history (linear):
```
* 10eda99 Local Dawn 2 (rebased)
* 4ec6c65 Remote Dawn 2
* 5bf9eb3 Dawn 1
```

- Easier to read logs (`git log`).
- Easier to bisect/debug.
- No unnecessary merge commits cluttering history.

---

## ⚡ When you don’t need `--force`
In your case, after you used `git reflog` + `git reset`, your local branch tip aligned in a way that made the push a **fast-forward**. That’s why you didn’t need `--force`. But that’s the exception — normally, rebase changes commit IDs, so force push is required.

---

## 🧭 Practical takeaway
- **Merge** → no force push, but messy history.  
- **Rebase** → usually needs force push, but gives clean history.  
- If you’re working alone, force push is safe.  
- If you’re collaborating, you need to coordinate (others must rebase too).  

---

👉 So the point of rebase is **not to avoid force pushing** — it’s to keep your history clean and linear. Force pushing is just the price you pay for that clarity.
